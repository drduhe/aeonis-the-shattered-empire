# Aeonis: The Shattered Empire — Lorebook

> *"The empire did not fall in a day. It fell in a single breath — and the world has spent a hundred years learning to breathe again."*
> — inscription above the Pilgrims' Gate, the Imperial Seat

This is the canonical setting book for **Aeonis: The Shattered Empire**. It expands the foundation laid in `rules_and_systems/Lore.md` and the eight Lord sheets in `lords/`. Everything here is flavor; rules live in `rules_and_systems/`.

---

## The Eternal Empire & the Shattering

The realm of Aeonis takes its name from **House Aeon**, the dynasty that raised the first walls of the Imperial Seat and ruled unbroken for more than two and a half thousand years. Scholars of the present age reckon time from that first stone.

### A timeline of the world

- **Year 0 — The Founding.** The First Emperor of House Aeon unites the warring river-kings of the central plains and lays the foundation of the Imperial Seat at the world's heart.
- **Years 0 – ~800 — The Age of Concord.** The provinces are bound by road, law, and levy. The High Council is chartered to govern what one throne cannot reach.
- **~800 – ~2300 — The Age of Wonders.** The empire's golden age. Imperial artisans carve the **Speaking Stones** from enchanted obsidian, letting the Emperor's council speak across a continent in a heartbeat. The great **portal network** is raised, gate by gate, until no province lies more than a day from the capital. Imperial outriders on the southern frontier bind wind spirits into their own bloodlines and become the first **Djinnborn**. It is also the age of the empire's great sins: the elven stewards of the old forests are betrayed and dispossessed by the Emperor's expansionist greed, and warlocks who traded in forbidden pacts are driven beyond the maps.
- **~2300 – ~2600 — The Waning.** Wonder curdles into hubris. To feed the ever-growing gate network and the court's hunger for miracles, imperial mages sink bore-shafts — the **Sap Wells** — into the roots of the **World Tree**, the living source of all magic, said to bridge the mortal and divine realms. In the empire's twilight years its spymasters experiment with dimensional phasing, and the first **Umbral** are born screaming into the spaces between light. The Tree's roots begin, quietly, to withdraw.
- **~2600 — The Shattering.** The last Emperor, wielding a fabled artifact said to grant immortality, orders the opening of a final, greatest gate — the **Great Gate** — meant to carry him across the threshold of death itself. The World Tree, drained past bearing, recoils. In a single cataclysm the balance between the mundane and the arcane breaks: magic gutters out across the world, the portal network detonates gate by gate, and the empire's cities fall in fire and silence. Where the Tree's sap spilled from the broken Sap Wells, the crystalline **Luminari** condense, carrying fragments of divine memory. Where the gate network's dimensional energy pools and congeals, the **Voidborn** coalesce from the wreckage, half-real and wholly strange. The Emperor vanishes from history. His name is stricken from every stone.
- **~2600 – ~2650 — The Age of Ash.** Darkness. Warlords, traitors, and forgotten gods tear the land asunder. Survival replaces ambition. The Speaking Stones scatter, silent.
- **~2650 – now (~Year 2700) — The Age of Lords.** A century after the fall, magic resurges — thin at first, then in floods. The scattered Speaking Stones begin to whisper again. And the banished lords return: exiles, outcasts, and the heirs of the empire's betrayed allies, each carrying a stone, each claiming a birthright, each turning toward the empty throne at the world's center.

The Shattering, then, was not an accident of history but a debt called due. The empire built its wonders on a living thing and drank it nearly dry; the Great Gate was merely the last swallow. Those who study the ruins say the same thing in a hundred ways: *the world did not break — it was spent.*

## The Speaking Stones

Every Lord in Aeonis carries a **Speaking Stone**: a fragment of ancient, enchanted obsidian carved by the empire's master artisans at the height of the Age of Wonders. In the old empire, the stones let the Emperor's council govern a continent — instantaneous speech across any distance, and more than speech: foresight, glimpses, the shapes of things not yet done.

When the empire fell, the stones went dark and were scattered across Aeonis. Their return to voice, a century later, marked the true beginning of the Age of Lords. No one agrees on how the stones choose their bearers. What is agreed is that a lord without a stone is merely a warlord, and a lord with one is a contender.

A Speaking Stone murmurs constantly to its keeper — fragments of the past, glimpses of the future, the moved troop, the bribed councilor, the assassin on the road. Lords call these fragments **whispers**, and wars are won on them. A timely whisper reveals an enemy's plan before it unfolds, or places a secret preparation exactly where it is needed. But the stones are a gift and a curse alike: they are conduits, and not everything that speaks through them is friendly. More than one lord has followed the whispers to greatness. More than one has followed them to madness, and could not afterward say where the one became the other.

The Umbral of the Silent Court insist the stones speak more freely to those born of shadow. Nyxara the Veiled puts it more simply: *"The stones tell me everything."*

## The Imperial Seat

At the exact center of Aeonis stands the **Imperial Seat**: a colossal city, the first city, raised at the Founding and never once conquered — only abandoned by history. Its outer rings still live. Pilgrims, merchants, scavengers, and spies crowd its avenues, and the High Council still convenes beneath its dome, a relic of imperial bureaucracy that survived the empire it served.

At the city's heart, past the Pilgrims' Gate and the mile of empty plinths where the statues of emperors were pulled down, the throne of the Eternal Empire sits vacant. It has been vacant for a hundred years. It is said that the throne confers what the last Emperor died — or vanished — reaching for: immortality, and the unarguable right to rule. Every claim to legitimacy in Aeonis runs through this room. Treaties are dated by its shadow; council titles derive from its charter; a lord who holds the Imperial Seat holds the argument that every other lord must answer.

And yet the throne remains empty, because the throne is cursed — or careful. Warlords have sat in it. Pretenders have crowned themselves before it. All of them died: by betrayal, by madness, by forces no witness could name, as if the empire itself were rejecting unworthy rulers. The priests of the World Tree say the Seat is not a prize but a judgment. The lords come anyway. Renown, armies, alliances, the recovered secrets of the old empire — all of it is spent toward one end: to be the claimant the throne does not kill.

## Geography: The Realm of Aeonis

Aeonis is a single sprawling landmass, its face rewritten by the Shattering. Ancient roads run arrow-straight into wilderness. Cities lie open to the sky. Every region below bears the empire's fingerprints — and its scars.

### The Crownfields

The great plains heartland surrounding the Imperial Seat: a sea of grass, grain, and half-buried monuments, the breadbasket of the old empire and of anyone who can hold it now. The Crownfields feed armies, and so armies never leave them alone. On the Sear's northern rim, where sand gives way to grass, the **Oasis Wellspring** waters the horse-markets where the Windsworn Horde trades — and from which it raids.

### The Long Steppe

East and north of the Crownfields the grass runs wild, beyond the reach of any lord's tax collector. The Long Steppe belongs to wandering clans, drovers' roads, and rumor. Imperial maps marked it simply *ungoverned*, which was true then and is truer now.

### The Ironveil Mountains

The northern spine of the world: iron, gold, and granite, veined with imperial mine-galleries and crowned with fortresses that never fell because no one bothered to climb to them. Vharok's **Iron Dominion** rules here from forge-lit bastions, and **Ironworks Ridge** rings day and night with the making of war. The deep galleries are older than the empire, and the miners do not go past the painted doors.

### The Duskspires

A range of black volcanic glass in the far west, where the peaks stand so close and sheer that whole valleys never see the sun. In the lightless galleries beneath them the Umbral hold their **Silent Court**, and the **Obsidian Spire** — the mountain from which the first Speaking Stones were quarried, some say — rises at their center like a needle through silk. Visitors are permitted. Their secrets are not permitted to leave.

### The Eldrenwood

The great elder forest of the northwest, old when the First Emperor was young. This was the heart of the elven stewards' realm before the empire's betrayal, and it is the seat of Elyndra's **Verdant Covenant** now. The deeper reaches close behind travelers like water. At the wood's heart stands the **Sacred Grove**, where the Covenant's oath was first sworn and is sworn still.

### The Radiant Vale

A forested vale in the southeast where the World Tree's presence breaks the surface of the world — groves that glow faintly at dusk, sap like liquid dawn, and the **Hallowed Grove** where the Luminari first opened their eyes. Auriel's **Radiant Accord** keeps the Vale as a living temple. Pilgrims of every race walk its white roads; armies think very carefully before marching on them.

### The Leywilds

The eastern forests grew back strangely after the Shattering, threading themselves along the empire's buried ley-conduits. Ruined colleges and observatories stand half-swallowed in the canopy, their libraries intact and lethally warded. Seraphel's **Arcanum Concord** claims the Leywilds, and the **Arcane Nexus** — a confluence of living ley lines — is its quiet, humming capital.

### The Sear

The vast southern desert. Its caravan rim, the **Amber Reach**, is a chain of market cities, toll roads, and counting houses where Cassian's **Golden Consortium** turns distance into profit; the **Caravan Bazaar** is its glittering hub, where anything may be bought, including most people. Beyond the rim lies the **Deep Sear** — dunes, glass plains, and heat-shimmer — which belongs to Rakhis's **Windsworn Horde** and always has, because nothing that cannot ride the wind survives it.

### The Mirrormarch

The lake country of the southwest: hundreds of cold, deep lakes strung like beads, their shores dotted with drowned imperial towns whose bell towers still break the surface. The lakes are barriers to armies and highways to those who know their waters. The barge-clans who do are not, for the moment, anyone's subjects.

### The Riftwood

In the far northeast a forest has grown up through the wreckage of the empire's greatest gate-hub, where the portal network was anchored and where it died loudest. Standing stones hum, distances misbehave, and shimmering figures walk between the trees. This is the home of Thal'rik's **Threshold**, and its **Rift Anchor** is the one great gate that still opens — for those the Gatekeeper permits.

### The Fallen Marches

Between the heartland and the old provinces runs a wide belt of ruin: shattered garrison towns, collapsed viaducts, vaults unopened since the Shattering. The Marches are where fortune-hunters go to get rich and where most of them stay forever. Beneath the Marches lies **Velmoth**, the imperial necropolis-city, sealed by the Council in the Age of Ash for reasons the minutes do not record.

## The Eight Banished Lords

They are called the Banished — those the empire cast out, and those its fall cast into being. Each carries a Speaking Stone. Each has a claim. None can win alone.

### Cassian, the Merchant Prince — Golden Consortium (Human)

*"Kingdoms fall to blades. Empires fall to ledgers."*

Cassian rose from the counting houses of the Amber Reach, where his family had spent three generations buying back, contract by contract, what the empire's collapse had taken from them. His armies are never the largest, but they are always where the contracts point. He buys access, sells protection, and turns every council vote into a margin. When war breaks out, he profits twice — once from the panic, and again from the peace that follows. His rivals call him a lord without a country. Cassian considers this an efficiency: countries are expensive, and everything they provide can be leased.

### Seraphel, Lord of the Arcane — The Arcanum Concord (Human)

*"The battlefield is only a diagram. I prefer to win with the first stroke of chalk."*

Seraphel's court in the Leywilds is a lattice of wards, lenses, and quiet bargains — heir to the imperial colleges whose masters were exiled in the Waning for asking what the Sap Wells were actually costing. Where other lords contest the map with force, Seraphel contests it with timing: the agenda seen before it opens, the battle chosen before it is offered. In the Arcanum Concord, knowledge is not power; it is *permission* — and every working, every ward, every whisper passes through Seraphel's hands on its way to being allowed.

### Vharok, the Lord of Steel — Iron Dominion (Human)

*"Strike the wall if you must. You will only learn what it costs to move the unmovable."*

The mountain garrisons of the Ironveil were the one imperial institution that did not fall at the Shattering, because they had been built to need nothing but stone, iron, and discipline — and Vharok's line has commanded them since the gates first shut. He rules from forge-lit bastions where every road is measured, every gate is trapped, and every war is fought on terms he has engineered. His armies do not race to victory; they deny it to others. The Dominion's creed is carved over every gate it holds: *steel does not tire.*

### Elyndra, Warden of Groves — Verdant Covenant (Elven)

*"You see trees. I see a thousand hands ready to pull you under."*

The elves of Aeonis are the realm's eldest people, bound by the **Covenant**: a living oath between the elven lines and the forest itself, sworn in the Sacred Grove before the empire existed. When the Emperor's expansionist greed betrayed the elven stewards and fed their outer woods to the axe, the Covenant endured it, root-patient — and when the empire fell, the forests began taking their territory back. Elyndra does not rule by decree; she rules by roots. Where others strip the land for immediate power, her domain grows denser, harder to invade, and increasingly self-sustaining. Invaders find their supply lines strangled and their formations broken by unseen growth. The Covenant has a long memory, and Elyndra is its patience given a voice.

### Rakhis, the Sandlord — The Windsworn Horde (Djinnborn)

*"The desert does not conquer. It simply outlasts everything that stands still."*

The Djinnborn are descendants of imperial outriders who, in the Age of Wonders, bound wind spirits into their own bloodlines — a bargain the empire sanctioned and then, characteristically, feared. Wind answers them like a second set of limbs; the deep desert that kills other travelers is simply weather to them. When the empire fell they retreated into the Deep Sear and thrived where others perished. Rakhis commands the largest mounted host in Aeonis — not through gold or titles, but through a simple promise: those who ride with him will never be cornered. His Horde moves like weather — visible on the horizon, impossible to catch, devastating when it arrives.

### Nyxara, the Veiled — The Silent Court (Umbral)

*"I heard your plan before you spoke it. The stones tell me everything."*

The Umbral are shadow-touched humanoids who dwell in the spaces between light, born from imperial spymasters who experimented with dimensional phasing in the empire's twilight years. Their skin shimmers like oil on water, and they perceive things hidden from mortal sight — the empire made them to keep its secrets, and they kept that skill when the empire could no longer keep them. Nyxara rules the Silent Court from no fixed throne; her seat of power is the network of whispers itself, spun outward from the Duskspires into every court and camp in Aeonis. She has never lost a war, because every war she fights is already over before the first blade is drawn.

### Auriel, Voice of the World Tree — The Radiant Accord (Luminari)

*"The Tree remembers every oath sworn and every oath broken. So do I."*

The Luminari are crystalline beings formed from the sap of the World Tree King during the empire's final cataclysm. Neither born nor made, they emerged fully conscious in the Radiant Vale, carrying fragments of divine memory — a hymn half-remembered, a law never written down, the sensation of two thousand years of prayers. They see themselves as the Tree's living will: its voice in a world that has forgotten how to listen. Auriel is the eldest of their kind, a being of radiant calm and terrifying conviction. She does not conquer; she consecrates. And those who stand on consecrated ground find it very difficult to stand against her.

### Thal'rik, the Gatekeeper — The Threshold (Voidborn)

*"You cross my gates because I allow it. Remember that."*

The Voidborn are not born — they coalesce. When the portal network collapsed at the Shattering, the dimensional energy that powered it did not dissipate; it pooled, congealed, and eventually became aware. The Voidborn emerged from the broken gates as beings of shimmering static, half-real and wholly strange. They remember nothing of the empire, yet the portal network hums in their veins like a mother tongue. Thal'rik is the first of his kind to claim sovereignty, and he considers the network his birthright — every gate a door in his house, from the Rift Anchor to the smallest wayside arch. He does not appreciate uninvited guests.

## Magic and the Arcane Schools

All magic in Aeonis flows, ultimately, from the World Tree. The empire's mages learned to draw on it the way a city draws on a river — and, like a city, never asked what happened upstream. At the Shattering the river simply stopped: workings failed mid-gesture, wards went cold, and a generation of practitioners woke up ordinary.

A century later, magic resurges. The Tree's roots are reaching back into the world — cautiously, unevenly, as if testing whether the world has learned anything. Ruins of the old empire hide the tomes, instruments, and forgotten knowledge that make the difference between a hedge-conjurer and a power, and the lords race to recover them, because those who command magic will shape the future of Aeonis.

Practitioners organize the recovered art into the old imperial schools:

- **Evocation**, the war magic — fire, force, and storm, the school the empire showed its enemies.
- **Enchantment**, the craft of wards and workings — the school that built what the empire kept.
- **Divination**, the art of knowing — beloved of councilors, spymasters, and everyone who has ever wanted to read a rival's letters unopened.
- **Transmutation**, the school of exchange — matter into matter, distance into nearness, expense into profit.
- **Geomancy**, the realmcraft — the shaping of land, border, and road; the school that raised the empire's wonders stone by stone.
- And **Necromancy**, forbidden then and forbidden now — the one school the empire outlawed even at its most arrogant. It is resurging anyway, in places where the other schools do not go.

But magic is fickle and dangerous, and its rediscovery has awoken dark beings who remember the empire's sins. Every lord who reaches for the old power must weigh the same question the empire never asked: *what happens upstream?*

## Artifacts & Remnants

The empire did not only leave ruins; it left its regalia. Scattered across Aeonis — in collapsed vaults, drowned towns, and the hoards of the Age of Ash — lie the **artifacts** of the Eternal Empire: enchanted weapons, ancient crowns, forbidden tomes, and warding stones, each unique, each imbued with divine or arcane power that the present age cannot replicate. The Blade of the Last Emperor. The Crown of Aeonis. The Shard of the Throne. To wield one is to borrow the empire's authority; to hold several is to start being spoken of in a different tone.

Far more common are **Remnants**: fragments of imperial workmanship — broken sigils, dead wardstones, coins of the old mint — that scavengers pry from ruins by the sackful. Individually they are curiosities. Gathered and studied, they lead somewhere: enough Remnants, in learned hands, have a way of becoming a map to something greater.

The artifacts are bound to the empire's fate, and their use draws attention — of rivals, and of the dark forces that remember what the empire did to make them. Some lords wield them as tools of conquest. Others quietly seek to destroy them, fearing what wakes when they are gathered.

## Faiths of Aeonis

The fall of the empire rekindled faith in the old gods, and one faith above all.

**The faith of the World Tree** holds that the **World Tree King** — the deity whose living roots extend across Aeonis, intertwining with the land and its people — governs the balance of life and magic, and that the Shattering was his judgment on an empire that drank from him without asking. Its rites are oaths, plantings, and pilgrimage; its holiest ground is the Radiant Vale; and its living voice, by the reckoning of most of the faithful, is Auriel of the Luminari, whose people are quite literally made of the god's blood. The faith preaches balance and stewardship. It is also, in Auriel's hands, a formidable political instrument — every oath sworn on the Tree is an oath the Accord may someday collect.

Against it stands the **Cult of the Undying Emperor**, the empire's old civic religion refusing to die with the empire. Its priests teach that the last Emperor did not perish at the Shattering but *crossed over* — that his final gate opened, that he waits beyond it deathless, and that he will return to reclaim his throne and judge those who sat in it. The Cult keeps shrines in the Imperial Seat's outer rings and among the Fallen Marches, and its congregations grow every year the throne stays empty. Where the Tree's faithful see the Shattering as judgment, the Cult sees it as *ascension interrupted* — and some of its inner circles are said to be working, with recovered Remnants, to finish what the Emperor started.

And in the shadows of both, darker gods lurk — forgotten powers of the Age of Ash, whispering promises to those desperate enough to listen. Some of those whispers come through Speaking Stones. Wise lords learn to tell the difference. Most lords believe they are wise.

## Legends & Mysteries

**The Nameless Emperor.** The last Emperor's name was stricken from every stone in the realm — and no living person can say who ordered it, or how it was accomplished in a single generation, or why no record so much as describes his face. He wielded an artifact said to grant immortality; he vanished from history; the throne he left kills its claimants. The Cult of the Undying Emperor says he is coming back. The unsettling thing, say the archivists of the Imperial Seat, is that nothing in the surviving records proves them wrong.

**Kaelthar, the Flamekeeper.** Before the empire, legend says, the Dragonkin kept great forges in the deep places of the world, and the last and greatest of their scions was Kaelthar, called the Flamekeeper. He appears in the histories of the Age of Wonders as ally, rival, and warning — then vanishes from them centuries before the Shattering, said to have sealed himself in the cold dragon forges to keep a fire the world was no longer worthy of. He is not among the lords who contend for the throne today. But with magic resurging, mountain villages from the Ironveil to the Sear's glass plains report the same rumor in the same words: *the forges are warm again.*

**The Skyflame Wyrm.** Highest of the old stories: a dragon whose fiery breath could level armies and whose scales no forged thing has pierced, seen — always at a distance, always at dusk — above the highest peaks of the world. The Age of Wonders recorded it. The Age of Ash prayed to it. The Age of Lords, being an age of ambitious fools, has already produced three expeditions to tame it. None returned. The Silent Court is said to pay well for confirmed sightings, and to pay better for silence about why it asks.

## Shadows on the Horizon

Not every power in Aeonis has yet shown its banner. Travelers and Speaking Stones alike carry rumors of forces still gathering at the edges of the age. Four of them now have names. *(These four contenders are designed as the expansion roster — see `../lords/Serathis.md`, `Morvane.md`, `Tsuvara.md`, and `Ozren.md`.)*

**The Drowned Court.** In the Mirrormarch, the drowned lake country, the barge-clans have long whispered of the **Tideborn** — the changed descendants of the coastal provinces the empire sank behind sorcerous floodwalls. The whispers were true. Their tide-priests read the waters the way the Arcanum reads its ley lines, and their queen, **Serathis the Tidecaller**, rules sunken halls no army can cross. The bell towers of the drowned towns have begun, on still nights, to ring.

**The Pale Legion.** The necropolis of Velmoth beneath the Fallen Marches was sealed in the Age of Ash, and the seals are old. Scavengers told of legionnaires in corroded imperial harness standing post at the buried gates — the Hollow Vigil, keeping an oath to an empire that no longer exists. Now the gates stand open. **Morvane the Deathless**, the empire's last Grand Marshal, has risen still wearing his rank, and every battlefield in Aeonis recruits for him. Necromancy is forbidden in Aeonis. Under Morvane's knotted banner, it is also *organized*.

**The Endless Caravan.** On the Long Steppe, the horn-crowned **Ashkarr** — the wagon-people who were the empire's roads — have always feuded too bitterly to matter beyond their grass. The feuding, traders report, has stopped. The nine Kessarin clans ride behind a single rolling city now: the Endless Caravan of **Tsuvara, Mother of Wagons**, which has never camped twice in the same place. Her outriders ask travelers a question that unsettles more the longer one thinks about it: *who holds the Imperial Seat, and by what right?*

**The Ashen Creed.** A heresy moves through the Tree-faith's pilgrim roads, preached in burn-scarred groves: that the World Tree is not healing but *rotting*, and that what rots must burn to be born again. Its prophet is **Ozren, the Unveiled Flame** — the priest the old faith condemned, whose pyre would not light. Three times. The Radiant Accord has declared the Creed anathema. The Creed does not appear to mind. Its preachers speak of a cleansing fire already kindled, and some of them, when they think no one is listening, speak the Flamekeeper's name.

*The Shattered Empire awaits its ruler. The stones are whispering. The roads are open. The throne is patient — and it is not the only thing that waits.*
