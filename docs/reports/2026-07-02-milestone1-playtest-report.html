Simulated Playtest Report — Milestone 1

Engine-authoritative simulator, first fuzz campaign, and rules-validation findings · July 2, 2026

TL;DR — Aeonis now has a working digital playtest engine. It played 300 complete games with zero crashes, every game fully replayable from a logged record. Encoding the rulebook into code surfaced 20 concrete rules ambiguities that need canon answers. Under random play, the Imperial Seat generates ~75% of all victory points and games end with an average winning margin of 7 VP — an early runaway-leader signal to test properly with strategic bots next.

1 · What was built

The simulator (sim/) is a faithful, coded implementation of the First Playable rules for 3–4 players: the full round structure, movement with zones of control and portals, recruiting, building, battle-line combat with sieges and Lord capture, production & upkeep, territory claims, objectives, and the 10 VP victory check. AI agents play through the same legal-move interface a human would use — the engine enumerates every legal action and rejects anything else, so rules violations are impossible by construction.

Every game writes a record containing the seed, every decision made, the verdict, and the final board — so any surprising result can be replayed move-for-move.

2 · Engine health: the fuzz campaign Clean

300 games (150 three-player, 150 four-player) were played by chaos bots — agents that pick uniformly random legal moves. Their job is to stress the rules engine, not to play well.

*Degenerate = the game finished but tripped a health monitor (e.g. long stretches with no VP progress). Timeout = hit the 25-round safety cap. Both are recorded findings, not failures.

Even under deliberately senseless play, ~95% of games reached a legitimate 10 VP victory in a plausible 6–25 rounds. The state-integrity checker (unit duplication, negative resources, phantom Lords, VP bookkeeping) ran after every single action across all 300 games without firing.

3 · Rules validation: 20 ambiguities found

Translating prose rules into code forces every vague sentence into the open. Each gap is logged in the Ambiguity Ledger with the interpretation the engine currently uses and which document should own the canonical answer. Highlights:

IDQuestion the rules don't answerEngine's interim ruling
AL-1Starting Population Cap math conflicts between the Packet (10) and Population.md (+3 per City)Base cap 7, so one starting City yields exactly 10
AL-5When are objectives claimed/scored? No timing window existsAuto-scored at Cleanup & Checks, once per card
AL-8What happens when Castle upkeep can't be paid?Castle effects suspend for the round; building persists
AL-9A captured Lord "returns to Home City" — what if it's enemy-held?Nearest safe controlled hex; stays captured if none
AL-12Do Archers strike again in the main Strike step after their Pre-Strike?No — Pre-Strike is their strike for the round
AL-15Can conquest push a player over the global Population cap?Yes — the cap only gates voluntary recruiting/building

The full ledger (20 entries) lives at playtest/Ambiguity_Ledger.md. Each “Open” entry is a small canon decision waiting to be made; several (AL-1, AL-5, AL-12) affect how the physical game should be taught.

4 · Early balance observations Caveats apply

Read this first: these numbers come from random-move bots. They say nothing about how skilled players will fare — but they do reveal what the game's structure rewards when nobody is steering. Treat everything below as hypotheses for the next phase, not conclusions.

  • The Imperial Seat is the whole economy. Seat income plus its streak bonus account for roughly 73% of every VP scored. Whoever camps the center rides it to 10 VP while opponents watch: the average winning margin was 7.3 VP (3p) and 6.9 VP (4p), and the most common margin was 8. In a 10-point game, that is a landslide.
  • Combat barely pays. Lord captures produced ~1% of VP, and the “win 2 battles” objective rarely landed. Random bots do attack — the reward, not the opportunity, is what's thin.
  • Seat position looks fair so far. Wins by seat: 38 / 50 / 55 in 3-player, 34 / 39 / 34 / 34 in 4-player — within noise for this sample. No first-player advantage visible yet.
  • Game length is healthy. Median ~12 rounds with a sensible 6–25 spread; the 25-round cap fired only twice in 300 games.

If the seat-dominance pattern survives contact with strategic bots, likely levers include the Seat's per-round VP rate, making the center costlier to hold uncontested, or raising the VP volume of combat and objectives.

5 · What's next

  • Persona bots (Warmonger, Economist, Expander, Diplomat) that pursue actual strategies — this is where real balance data starts, including win-rate matrices and runaway-leader detection with statistical weight.
  • Automated balance reports aggregated across thousand-game tournaments.
  • Canon triage of the 20 ledger entries — each is a quick ruling that tightens the rulebook before the next physical playtest.
  • Later: LLM agents that read the actual rulebook and report confusion the way human playtesters would, plus expanded content (Strategy cards, Council votes, Whispers, Lords).
